## The Intemann Trail: Views Without the Crowds
<a name="the-intemann-trail-views-without-the-crowds"></a>

While thousands of people are sweating their way up the Manitou Incline, savvy locals are often just a stone's throw away on the Intemann Trail, enjoying spectacular views with a fraction of the foot traffic. This rolling singletrack trail connects Manitou Springs to the west side of Colorado Springs, offering a fantastic alternative perspective of the same dramatic landscape.

### Why It's a Hidden Gem
The Intemann Trail is one of the best **hidden gems for hiking in CO Springs** because it exists in the shadow of giants. Flanked by Garden of the Gods, Red Rock Canyon, and the Incline, it's easily overlooked. The trail contours along the foothills, dipping in and out of forested ravines and opening up to expansive vistas of the city and the plains beyond. You get all the classic Colorado scenery—yucca, scrub oak, and towering pines—without the conga line of hikers.

### Trail Details
*   **Location**: Multiple access points. A great place to start is from the Iron Spring trailhead in Manitou Springs or from Section 16 trailhead off Gold Camp Road.
*   **Length**: The full trail is ~6.5 miles one-way, but it's easily broken into shorter out-and-back sections.
*   **Difficulty**: Moderate. There are plenty of ups and downs, but no single climb is overwhelmingly long or steep.
*   **Best For**: Trail runners, hikers looking for a solid half-day adventure, and anyone wanting Pikes Peak views without the Pikes Peak crowds.

### Local's Tip
For a rewarding 3-mile round-trip hike, start at the Section 16 trailhead and head east (towards the city) on the Intemann Trail. You'll quickly climb to a ridge with a perfectly placed bench that offers one of the best photo ops of Garden of the Gods framed by Pikes Peak. It feels like a secret lookout spot made just for you.

## Columbine Trail: A North Cheyenne Cañon Gem
<a name="columbine-trail-a-north-cheyenne-cañon-gem"></a>

North Cheyenne Cañon Park is a city-owned treasure, but most visitors rarely venture beyond the main road, Helen Hunt Falls, or the popular Seven Bridges Trail. Tucked away on the north side of the canyon, the Columbine Trail offers a more challenging and secluded journey deep into the woods.

### Why It's a Hidden Gem
This trail is for those who don't mind earning their solitude. It’s a steady, heart-pumping climb that quickly lifts you out of the bustling canyon floor and into a serene, high-country environment. The trail is well-maintained but rugged in sections, which deters many casual walkers. Your reward is the peaceful sound of your own footsteps, the scent of pine, and stunning overhead views of the canyon you just left behind.

### Trail Details
*   **Location**: Starts at the Starsmore Discovery Center parking lot at the entrance of North Cheyenne Cañon Park (2120 S Cheyenne Canyon Rd, Colorado Springs, CO 80906).
*   **Length**: Approximately 4 miles one-way to Helen Hunt Falls, but many hikers turn around at the junction with the Spring Creek trail for a satisfying 4.5-mile round trip.
*   **Difficulty**: Strenuous. The trail gains over 1,500 feet of elevation.
*   **Best For**: Fit hikers, trail runners looking for a vertical challenge, and autumn leaf-peeping (the aspens here are spectacular).

### Local's Tip
Start early to get a parking spot at Starsmore and to enjoy the cool morning air on your climb. The trail is mostly shaded, making it a great option on warmer days. ## Austin Bluffs Open Space: An Urban Oasis
<a name="austin-bluffs-open-space-an-urban-oasis"></a>

You don't always have to drive to the mountains to find a great hike. Located right in the heart of Colorado Springs near the University of Colorado Colorado Springs (UCCS) campus, Austin Bluffs is a 584-acre geological wonderland that flies completely under the tourist radar. It’s a labyrinth of sandstone formations, small canyons, and scenic overlooks.

### Why It's a Hidden Gem
This is a true **local trail in Colorado Springs**. Its urban location makes it a go-to for nearby residents, but visitors rarely know it exists. The park features unique rock formations called "hoodoos" that feel like a miniature version of Bryce Canyon. Because it's a network of interconnecting trails rather than a single destination, it's easy to find your own quiet corner to explore.

### Trail Details
*   **Location**: Main parking is at University Park or Pulpit Rock Park (3320 N Nevada Ave, Colorado Springs, CO 80907).
*   **Length**: Varies. You can do a quick 1-mile loop or link trails for a 5+ mile adventure.
*   **Difficulty**: Easy to moderate. The main trails are wide and gentle, but you can find steeper, more technical side trails to explore.
*   **Best For**: Families, dog walkers, sunset hikes, and anyone looking for a quick nature fix without leaving the city.

### Local's Tip
The most famous formation is Pulpit Rock, which is worth the short, steep climb for a 360-degree view of the city and mountain range. For a quieter experience, explore the trails on the eastern side of the park, which wind through intriguing canyons and bluffs with far fewer people.

## Blodgett Peak Open Space: The Local's Workout
<a name="blodgett-peak-open-space-the-locals-workout"></a>

If you ask a Colorado Springs local for a genuinely tough hike that isn't Pikes Peak, they'll likely point you toward Blodgett Peak. This prominent peak on the northwest side of town offers a grueling but immensely rewarding climb. It's a rite of passage for serious local hikers.

### Why It's a Hidden Gem
Blodgett is a secret in plain sight. Everyone can see it, but its reputation for being steep and rugged keeps the crowds at bay. There’s no gentle warm-up here; the trail starts climbing immediately and doesn't let up. The final push involves some scrambling (using your hands to navigate rocks), which makes it an adventure that’s off-limits to most tourists.

### Trail Details
*   **Location**: Trailhead is at the end of Woodmen Road (3780 W Woodmen Rd, Colorado Springs, CO 80919).
*   **Length**: ~3.5 miles round-trip.
*   **Difficulty**: Extremely Strenuous. This is a short trail, but it packs in nearly 2,000 feet of elevation gain.
*   **Best For**: Experienced and very fit hikers comfortable with route-finding and scrambling. This is not a trail for beginners or children.

### Local's Tip
This is a serious mountain hike. Start very early, bring more water than you think you'll need (at least 3 liters), and pack the "10 Essentials" (navigation, sun protection, layers, etc.). The trail can be tricky to follow after a washed-out section, so using a GPS app like AllTrails is highly recommended. The summit views, stretching from Denver to the Spanish Peaks, are absolutely worth the effort.

## Section 16 & Palmer Loop: A Backyard Epic
<a name="section-16--palmer-loop-a-backyard-epic"></a>

Nestled between Bear Creek Cañon Park, Red Rock Canyon, and Manitou Springs, this area contains a fantastic loop that locals love. The Section 16/Palmer Loop combines two trails to create a perfect half-day hike with a little bit of everything: steep climbs, gentle meadows, dense forest, and wide-open views.

### Why It's a Hidden Gem
While the trailhead is popular, most people do a simple out-and-back on one of the trails. Committing to the full 6-mile loop thins the crowd significantly. You'll feel like you’ve gone on a true backcountry adventure, yet you’re just minutes from the city. It’s the perfect synthesis of convenience and wilderness.

### Trail Details
*   **Location**: Trailhead is located off Gold Camp Road. From Hwy 24, take 21st Street south, which turns into Cresta Road. Turn right on Gold Camp Road and follow it to the parking area on the right.
*   **Length**: 5.7-mile loop.
*   **Difficulty**: Moderate to Strenuous. The loop involves about 1,300 feet of elevation gain.
*   **Best For**: Hikers looking for a good workout, amazing city views, and a trail that changes character around every bend.

### Local's Tip
Hike the loop counter-clockwise. This gets the steepest, most grueling section (the "incline" portion of Section 16) out of the way first, leaving you with a long, beautiful, and more gradual descent through the forest on the Palmer Trail. ## Essential Tips for Hiking Like a Local
<a name="essential-tips-for-hiking-like-a-local"></a>

To make the most of these **secret hikes in Colorado Springs**, you need to be prepared. The weather can change in an instant, and the altitude is no joke. Here are a few tips to keep you safe and happy on the trail.

### Acclimatize to the Altitude
Colorado Springs sits at over 6,000 feet above sea level. If you're coming from a lower elevation, give yourself a day or two to adjust before tackling a strenuous trail. Drink lots of water—more than you think you need—and listen to your body. Headaches and shortness of breath are signs to take it easy.

### Check the Weather and Start Early
Afternoon thunderstorms are a staple of Colorado summers. They can build quickly and bring lightning, hail, and dramatic temperature drops. Locals know to start their hikes early in the morning to be off the summit or exposed ridges by the early afternoon. Always check the forecast before you go.

### What to Pack
Even for a short hike, it's wise to carry the "Ten Essentials." This includes:
1.  **Navigation**: Map, compass, or a GPS app on your fully charged phone.
2.  **Sun Protection**: Sunscreen, sunglasses, and a hat. The sun is intense at altitude.
3.  **Insulation**: Extra layers (like a fleece or rain jacket), even on a warm day.
4.  **Illumination**: A headlamp or flashlight.
5.  **First-Aid Supplies**.
6.  **Fire Starter**: Lighter or waterproof matches.
7.  **Repair Kit**: Multi-tool or knife.
8.  **Nutrition**: Extra food (beyond your planned lunch).
9.  **Hydration**: Extra water.
10. **Emergency Shelter**: An emergency blanket.

### Leave No Trace
The golden rule of the outdoors is to leave it better than you found it. Pack out everything you pack in (including food scraps and dog waste), stay on designated trails to prevent erosion, and respect wildlife by observing from a distance.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Hiking in Colorado Springs
<a name="frequently-asked-questions-about-hiking-in-colorado-springs"></a>

### What is the least crowded hiking trail in Colorado Springs?
While no trail is ever guaranteed to be empty, some of the least crowded options are typically the more challenging ones or those located in less-famous parks. A trail like the Columbine Trail in North Cheyenne Cañon or the upper loops at Austin Bluffs Open Space will offer far more solitude than anything in Garden of the Gods.

### Are there any easy hidden hiking trails in Colorado Springs?
Yes! For an easy but quiet experience, explore the network of trails at Ute Valley Park (not to be confused with Ute Trail on Pikes Peak). The flat, wide trails on the western side of the park offer lovely views and are great for families, but they see far fewer tourists than the more central parks.

### Do you need a permit to hike on local trails in Colorado Springs?
For the vast majority of trails within city and county parks and open spaces (like all the ones listed in this guide), you do not need a permit or pass. Parking is also free at these trailheads. The main exceptions would be State Parks (like Cheyenne Mountain State Park) or federal lands in the Pike National Forest, which may require a parks pass or fee.

### What's the best time of year for hiking in Colorado Springs?
You can hike year-round in Colorado Springs, which is one of its greatest appeals!
*   **Spring (April-May)**: Wildflowers begin to bloom, but trails can be muddy from snowmelt.
*   **Summer (June-August)**: The most popular season. Start early to avoid afternoon thunderstorms and the heat.
*   **Fall (September-October)**: Arguably the best season. The weather is cool and stable, the aspens turn a brilliant gold, and the trails are less crowded.
*   **Winter (November-March)**: Lower elevation trails are often accessible, though they can be icy. Microspikes or traction devices for your shoes are highly recommended.
