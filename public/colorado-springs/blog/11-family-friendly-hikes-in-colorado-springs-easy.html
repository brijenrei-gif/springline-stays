11 Family-Friendly Hikes in Colorado Springs: Easy Trails with Big Views

2024-05-20 • 6 min read
Home / Colorado Springs Stays / 11 Family-Friendly Hikes in Colorado Springs: Easy Trails with Big Views

```

If you’re trying to hike Garden of the Gods at 10:00 AM on a Saturday, you’re going to spend more time circling the parking lot than actually looking at the red rocks. The secret to enjoying Colorado Springs’ most iconic trails with kids in tow is knowing exactly where to park and which “shortcut” leads to the best overlook without a 400-foot elevation gain.

While the Incline gets all the social media glory, the real magic of the Pikes Peak region is found on the mellow loops where the toddlers can roam and the grandparents can actually catch their breath.

1. Perkins Central Garden Trail (Garden of the Gods)

This is the “paved and easy” one, but don’t let the crowds deter you. It puts you directly underneath the 300-foot towering sandstone fins. * Local Tip: Park in the main lot (Parking Lot 2) before 8:30 AM. If it’s full, head to the Trading Post and walk in from the south side—it’s much quieter. * The View: Looking through the “Kissing Camels” rock formation.

2. Trading Post Trail (Red Rock Canyon Open Space)

Often overshadowed by its neighbor (Garden of the Gods), Red Rock Canyon is where locals actually go. The Trading Post Trail is a 1.5-mile loop that weaves through dramatic rock formations and offers plenty of flat spots for little legs. * Local Tip: Stop by the “old quarry” area. It looks like a giant natural amphitheater and is the perfect spot for a mid-hike snack.

3. Seven Bridges Trail (North Cheyenne Cañon)

This is the ultimate “adventure” hike for kids. As the name suggests, you cross seven numbered wooden bridges over a bubbling creek. The trail is shaded by massive evergreens, making it perfect for mid-summer. * Local Tip: Most families stop at Bridge 7 and turn around. The trail gets significantly steeper after that, so Bridge 7 is the logical endpoint for a stress-free afternoon.

4. Grandview Trail (Palmer Park)

Palmer Park is a massive plateau in the middle of the city. The Grandview Trail offers exactly what it promises: an unobstructed view of Pikes Peak and the entire Front Range. * Local Tip: Head here for sunset. There’s a playground near the trailhead (Maizeland entrance) where the kids can burn off their final energy while you watch the peak turn pink.

5. Sand Canyon Loop (Red Rock Canyon Open Space)

If you want to feel like you’re in a miniature Grand Canyon, this is it. It’s sandy, flat, and features high canyon walls that provide some shade in the mornings. * Local Tip: Keep an eye out for the “sand waves” in the rock—remnants of the prehistoric ocean that once covered Colorado.

6. The Paint Mines Interpretive Park

Located about 45 minutes east of the city in Calhan, this looks like another planet. The white, pink, and purple clay hoodoos are stunning. * Local Tip: It is almost always windy here. Bring a light windbreaker even if it’s hot in town. Also, do not climb on the formations—the rangers are strict, and the clay is fragile.

7. Helen Hunt Falls & Silver Cascade

You can literally drive to the base of Helen Hunt Falls, but take the short 1/3-mile hike up to Silver Cascade Falls. It’s a bit of a stair-climber, but it’s over in 10 minutes and gives you a bird’s-eye view of the canyon. * Local Tip: The parking lot is tiny. If it’s full, park further down North Cheyenne Cañon Road and walk the “Lower Gold Camp” path up.

8. Ute Valley Park Loop

This is a favorite for local families with strollers (all-terrain ones work best here). The white sandstone ridges provide a beautiful contrast to the green pines. * Local Tip: Grab a post-hike beer or soda at Goat Patch Brewing right down the road—it’s extremely family-friendly.

9. Fountain Creek Nature Trail

Located south of the city in Fountain, this is a “hidden gem” for wildlife viewing. You’ll likely spot deer, turkeys, and plenty of waterfowl along the easy, flat marshes. * Local Tip: Visit the Nature Center first. They have great “discovery packs” for kids to use while on the trail.

10. Dixon Trail Lower Loop (Cheyenne Mountain State Park)

State parks in Colorado require a pass, but it’s worth it for the pristine trail conditions and clean bathrooms. The lower loops are wide and offer great views of the “backside” of the Broadmoor area. * Local Tip: This park is a hotspot for black bears and turkeys. Keep your eyes peeled on the scrub oak thickets.

11. Fox Run Regional Park (The Lake Loop)

Located in the northern part of the city (Black Forest), this park is heavily wooded with Ponderosa pines. The Lake Loop is flat, circles two small ponds, and offers one of the best framed views of Pikes Peak in the region. * Local Tip: There are multiple “forest” themed playgrounds here. If you need a break from hiking, the northern playground is usually the least crowded.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time of year to hike in Colorado Springs?

June through September offers the most reliable weather, but don’t sleep on “Indian Summer” in October. If you hike in the winter or spring, bring micro-spikes or Yaktrax; trails in North Cheyenne Cañon stay icy long after the snow has melted in the city.

Do I need a permit or pass for these hikes?

Most city parks (Garden of the Gods, Palmer Park, Red Rock Canyon) are free. Cheyenne Mountain State Park requires a daily vehicle pass ($10), and the Paint Mines are free but have limited hours.

Are these trails stroller-friendly?

Perkins Central Garden is fully paved. Fountain Creek and Fox Run are manageable with a jogging stroller. For Seven Bridges or the Paint Mines, you’ll definitely want a child-carrier backpack instead.
