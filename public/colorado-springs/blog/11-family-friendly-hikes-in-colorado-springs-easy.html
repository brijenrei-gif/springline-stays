11 Family-Friendly Hikes in Colorado Springs: Easy Trails with Big Views

2026-04-08 • 11 min read
Home / Colorado Springs Stays / 11 Family-Friendly Hikes in Colorado Springs: Easy Trails with Big Views

Let’s be honest: hiking with kids can either be a magical core memory or a complete disaster. The difference is usually the trail you choose. Forget those grueling, high-elevation slogs. You’re looking for that perfect sweet spot—a hike that’s short enough for little legs, interesting enough to prevent the dreaded “I’m bored,” and delivers a scenic payoff that makes everyone feel like a true adventurer.

Here are 11 tried-and-true family hikes in Colorado Springs that deliver exactly that. These are the trails we take our own families on, the ones that guarantee big smiles and even bigger views.

1. Garden of the Gods: Perkins Central Garden Trail

This is the quintessential Colorado Springs experience for a reason. The main loop through the heart of the park is paved, wide, and winds directly through the most iconic, massive red rock formations. It’s less of a “hike” and more of a spectacular walk.

Why It’s Great for Kids

It’s 100% stroller and wheelchair accessible. The towering rocks are like a natural jungle gym (for the eyes, mostly!), and the path is so easy that toddlers can wander freely without you worrying about steep drop-offs.

Local Tip

Parking here is famously tricky. The secret is to go either super early (before 9 AM) or in the late afternoon for golden hour when the crowds thin out. If the main lot is full, don’t circle endlessly. Park at the Garden of the Gods Trading Post and walk in from there—you can grab a well-deserved ice cream cone on your way out.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 1.5-mile loop
  • Elevation Gain: Minimal (less than 30 feet)
  • Difficulty: Easiest

2. Red Rock Canyon Open Space: Quarry Pass Trail

Think of Red Rock Canyon as Garden of the Gods’ slightly wilder, less-crowded cousin. The Quarry Pass Trail takes you up a gentle incline to a beautiful view overlooking a serene pond and the towering rock walls. There are plenty of fun, smaller rocks for kids to scramble on along the way.

Why It’s Great for Kids

It feels like a real adventure without being too strenuous. The trail is wide and well-marked, and the reward-to-effort ratio is fantastic. Plus, there’s a good chance you’ll see rock climbers, which is always a thrill.

Local Tip

The main parking lot off Highway 24 can get busy. Try the secondary lot off 31st Street for easier access to a different, equally fun trail network. Afterward, drive 5 minutes to Old Colorado City and grab a slice of pizza at Pizzeria Rustica—their patio is fantastic.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 1.2-mile loop
  • Elevation Gain: ~200 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

3. Paint Mines Interpretive Park

This place is pure magic. Located about 45 minutes east of the Springs, the Paint Mines feel like another planet. You’ll hike down into a canyon filled with colorful clay formations, hoodoos, and spires in brilliant shades of orange, purple, and white.

Why It’s Great for Kids

It’s a natural playground that sparks the imagination. The formations are incredible, and kids love exploring the narrow passages and washes. It’s an easy walk down, and you can explore as much or as little as you want.

Local Tip

There is absolutely no shade here. Avoid going in the middle of a hot summer day. An overcast day is actually perfect for bringing out the colors in the clay for photos. Also, please remind your kids (and yourselves!) to stay on the trails and not climb on the fragile formations.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 3.4-mile loop (but you can do much less)
  • Elevation Gain: ~350 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

4. Helen Hunt Falls

Who doesn’t love a waterfall? This is one of the easiest to access in the entire region. It’s a very short, slightly steep walk from the parking lot to the bridge at the base of the falls.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The payoff is immediate. You can hear the water from the car, and the short path builds excitement. The mist coming off the falls is a huge hit on a warm day.

Local Tip

Don’t stop at the bridge! Take the stairs up the side of the falls for a different perspective and a short, fun trail at the top called the “Silver Cascade Falls Trail.” It adds about 10-15 minutes to your hike and gets you away from the main crowd.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 0.1 miles to the main viewpoint
  • Elevation Gain: ~50 feet
  • Difficulty: Easiest

5. Palmer Park: Grandview Overlook

Sitting right in the middle of the city, Palmer Park is a geological anomaly and a local favorite. A network of sandy trails weaves through cactus and rock formations. The easiest and most rewarding path for families is the paved scenic road up to the Grandview Overlook.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The views are epic. You can see the entire Front Range, from Pikes Peak to the Air Force Academy. It’s a great spot to have a picnic and let the kids run around the relatively flat mesa top.

Local Tip

Drive to the top and park, then explore the short dirt trails that splinter off from the overlook. The one heading south from the main overlook gives you incredible views of the city with Pikes Peak as the backdrop.

Trail Details

  • Distance: Varies, but the trail from the overlook is ~0.5 miles
  • Elevation Gain: Minimal from the top parking lot
  • Difficulty: Easy

6. Bear Creek Nature Center Trails

Perfect for the littlest hikers. The trails here are flat, wide, and meander along Bear Creek. The nature center itself is a fantastic starting point, with interactive exhibits and clean bathrooms.

Why It’s Great for Kids

This is toddler paradise. The trails are smooth enough for a rugged stroller, and there’s always something to see, from birds and prairie dogs to the babbling creek. It’s more of a nature walk than a hike.

Local Tip

Pack a picnic. There are fantastic, shady picnic tables right by the creek just a short walk from the nature center. It’s the perfect place to refuel after your “big” hike.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 1-2 miles of interconnected loops
  • Elevation Gain: Flat
  • Difficulty: Easiest

7. Fox Run Regional Park

Located north of the city in Black Forest, this park is a welcome escape on hot days. The main 2.5-mile loop winds through a fragrant Ponderosa Pine forest, offering plenty of shade.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The trail is a mix of packed dirt and small hills, making it feel like a real hike. Plus, the park has two amazing playgrounds and a lake where kids can watch the ducks.

Local Tip

Start your hike near the main playground on the west side of the park. That way, the playground serves as the perfect bribe for finishing the loop.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 2.5-mile loop
  • Elevation Gain: ~200 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

8. Seven Bridges Trail

This one is a true family adventure. The trail follows North Cheyenne Creek and, as the name suggests, you get to cross seven wooden bridges. It’s almost entirely shaded and incredibly beautiful.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The bridges are the star. Kids love counting them as they go, which keeps them motivated. The sound of the creek and the fun of rock-hopping make this a favorite.

Local Tip

Parking is the biggest challenge here. The small lot at the trailhead fills up extremely early. Don’t even try it on a weekend morning. Go on a weekday, or park lower down in North Cheyenne Cañon Park and add a bit of a walk up Gold Camp Road to the trailhead. It’s worth the effort.

Trail Details

  • Distance: ~3.5 miles out-and-back (can turn around anytime)
  • Elevation Gain: ~800 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy to Moderate

9. Ute Valley Park

This is a local’s gem on the northwest side of town. Ute Valley offers a fantastic network of trails with sandstone cliffs, ridges, and sweeping views of Pikes Peak.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The geology is fascinating, with lots of nooks and crannies to explore. It’s a popular spot for dog walkers, so it’s great if your family includes a four-legged member. The main loop is challenging enough to feel rewarding for older kids.

Local Tip

Watch for mountain bikers, as this is a shared-use park. The western loop offers the best views for the least amount of effort. For a post-hike treat, hit up Josh & John’s Ice Cream on the way back into town.

Trail Details

  • Distance: Varies, loops from 1-4 miles
  • Elevation Gain: ~300 feet on the main loop
  • Difficulty: Easy

10. Cheyenne Mountain State Park: Zook Loop

State parks offer incredibly well-maintained trails, and Cheyenne Mountain is no exception. The Zook Loop is a gentle, rolling trail through grasslands with a high probability of spotting wildlife like deer or wild turkeys.

Why It’s Great for Kids

The trails are wide, smooth, and perfect for kids who are just getting used to hiking. The visitor center is also a great resource with clean bathrooms and helpful rangers.

Local Tip

There is a day-use fee to enter the park, but it’s worth it for the pristine conditions and amenities. Pack binoculars! The open terrain makes it a fantastic place for wildlife spotting.

Trail Details

  • Distance: 1-mile loop
  • Elevation Gain: ~100 feet
  • Difficulty: Easy

11. Pulpit Rock Park

For slightly older kids who want a “summit,” Pulpit Rock is perfect. It’s a short but steep climb up a prominent rock formation that offers incredible 360-degree views of the city and Pikes Peak.

Why It’s Great for Kids

It’s a quick, high-reward hike that makes kids feel like they’ve conquered a mountain. The scramble at the top is fun and adventurous for ages 6 and up.

Local Tip

The trail can be slippery due to loose gravel. Make sure everyone has shoes with good grip. The best photos aren’t from the very top, but from the flat ledge just below the final scramble, looking south toward Pikes Peak.

Trail Details

  • Distance: ~0.7 miles out-and-back to the top
  • Elevation Gain: ~275 feet
  • Difficulty: Moderate (due to steepness)

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the absolute easiest, most scenic hike in Colorado Springs for toddlers?

The Perkins Central Garden Trail in Garden of the Gods is unbeatable for toddlers. It’s a 1.5-mile paved loop with no elevation gain, making it perfect for strollers and wobbly walkers. The views are world-class, with giant red rocks towering right next to the path.

Are the trails in Garden of the Gods stroller-friendly?

Yes! The main Perkins Central Garden Trail is a paved, concrete loop that is fully accessible for strollers, wheelchairs, and wagons. Many of the other smaller trails branching off from the main path are also wide and relatively flat, though they may be gravel instead of pavement.

Do I need to worry about altitude sickness on these easy hikes?

While Colorado Springs is at a high elevation (~6,035 feet), it’s unlikely you or your children will experience serious altitude sickness on these short, low-exertion hikes. The most common symptoms are mild headaches or feeling out of breath. The key is to stay hydrated—drink more water than you think you need, starting the day before your hike.
